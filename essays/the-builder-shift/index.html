The Builder Shift

An attempt to rationalize a changing landscape, and a case for why everyone who builds software might need the same job title.

The design landscape is changing and I've been trying to make sense of it. I keep looking backward, at how we ended up here, to figure out whether our current positions actually hold.

I don't think they do. The jobs of building software are going to change fundamentally. All of them, design included. This is my attempt at a sober take on why, and where I think it lands: a convergence toward a single role. The builder.

· · ·

why design exists

For a long time, engineers built the thing and decided what it looked like. When projects started failing regularlyThe Software CrisisBy the 1960s, software projects were routinely late, over budget, and broken. The 1968 NATO conference in Garmisch coined the term and pushed the industry toward formal engineering practices.wikipedia ↗, specs were invented for accountability. Then GUIsGraphical User InterfacesThe shift from command-line to graphical interfaces began in the 1970s at Xerox PARC and went mainstream with the Macintosh in 1984 and Windows in 1985.wikipedia ↗ showed up and suddenly software had screens real people had to figure out. Design arrived because someone needed to sit with the human at the other end. Two things defined the lane:

  • Direction. A human-centered lens on what to build. The practice of being wrong cheaply before being wrong expensively.
  • Craft. The discipline of caring about the gap between functional and good.

Design was always additive. A quality multiplier on top of the engineering that actually shipped the product. Designers were grossly outnumbered from the start, and that ratio was always telling.

· · ·

on direction

Direction was always the harder problem. What should we build? Who is it for? Christopher AlexanderChristopher AlexanderArchitect and design theorist. His 1964 book "Notes on the Synthesis of Form" argued that design is the process of achieving fit between a form and its context. His later "A Pattern Language" influenced fields from architecture to software engineering.wikipedia ↗ called design the process of achieving fit between a form and its context. The form is what you're making. The context is the problem. Design answered this through research, feedback loops, and iteration, constantly testing whether the thing being built actually fit the world it was going into. Design systemsDesign SystemsReusable components, standards, and documentation that guide consistent product development. Examples: Material Design, Lightning, Fluent.wikipedia ↗ scaled the answers by encoding affordances, accessibility defaults, and interaction patterns into shared infrastructure.

Anyone close enough to the problem can provide direction now. PMs do it. Engineers do it. The tools make it easier for everyone to act on what they see. So the question evolves. In a builder world, direction becomes focus. When everyone can build anything, the hard part is deciding what not to build.

· · ·

on craft

The craft of design has been changing. Production design is increasingly supported by design systems that grow more complex and capable every year. The floor keeps getting lower and the ceiling keeps getting raised. Craft, in the traditional sense, is becoming table stakes.

So what comes after craft? I think it's style. Taste is relative and cultural. It doesn't have age restrictions or prerequisites. There are no barriers to it. Anyone can have it. But cultivating it takes time and exposure, and it's the thing that separates someone who can use the tools from someone who knows what to do with them. In a builder world, style is what you carry forward when everything else gets easier.

· · ·

the convergence

This is also an economic story. The ZIRP eraZIRP EraFrom roughly 2009–2022, near-zero interest rates fueled cheap capital, aggressive hiring, and a culture of experimentation. The rapid rate hikes of 2022–2023 ended it abruptly.wikipedia ↗ promoted specialization. Cheap capital meant you could fund a UX researcher, a visual designer, an interaction designer, a content strategist, all on the same team. That era is over. The pressure now runs toward T-shaped people who cover more ground.

PM, design, and engineering still exist as specialties, and everyone has more agency over each other's domain. The boundaries blur because the tools let everyone act on what they see. The act of production is getting solved daily. The gap is in creation. The thinking, the decisions, the trade-offs.

Designers aren't going away. We're becoming the first wave of a much larger pool of people who build software. The title changes. The verb doesn't.
· · ·

the builder

A builder works with extremely high agency. They don't wait for a ticket or a spec. They're closer to an internal entrepreneur who accepts failures alongside wins, learns from both, and iterates. They use every kind of intelligence available to solve problems. Their own, their teammates', their tools'. Some builders will focus on design. They might still be called designers. They'll just be owning outcomes instead of outputs.

And because production is cheap, because you can will things into existence almost as fast as you can think of them, restraint becomes a craft in itself. A good builder is as careful about what they don't ship as they are ambitious about what they do. We've never suffered from a shortage of people who can make things. We've suffered from not knowing what to build. That problem just got louder.

The constraint was never "can we build it." It was always "should we."
· · ·

I want to be honest about what this is. It's my attempt to rationalize change I find unsettling. I'm not saying our current way of working is broken. We can continue how we have been. But I think doing so means leaving potential on the table, and there's an opportunity to embrace something new.

I'm not saying a title change fixes everything either. But a shift in mindset, in how we think about our responsibilities and what we're capable of, might. Change is happening fast and it's disorienting for everyone. I think the best response is to try to shape your own world. Build out what you think it is. Rationalize it as best you can, and then adjust your thinking as you learn more. I'll do the same. That's what any good designer would do.
